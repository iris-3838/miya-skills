# Research Question Architecture Patterns

Use this reference during Phase 1 scoping when a project has both a concrete focal debate/case and a broader theoretical ambition.

## Pattern: dual-focus, asymmetrically anchored RQ

When the user wants both:

1. a substantive RQ about a bounded focal object (e.g. Floridi's Philosophy of Information in LIS), and
2. a broader meta-theoretical RQ (e.g. how LIS theory relates to methodology/paradigms),

do not force either of these simplistic structures:

- "the focal object is merely a representative case" — this can incorrectly demote the user's main research target.
- "the two RQs are fully co-equal/parallel" — this can overstate symmetry if the evidence base is primarily anchored in the focal object.

Prefer a careful formulation:

> This is a dual-focus study. The first focus is the bounded focal debate itself. The second focus is the broader theoretical question opened by that debate. The two foci are not in a simple main/subordinate relation, but the evidence base may be asymmetrically anchored in the focal corpus; broader claims should therefore be framed as analytical or typological implications rather than exhaustive generalizations.

## Recommended RQ shape

- RQ1 (bounded/focal): How has [the focal theory/debate] been presented, received, criticized, and reappraised within [field]?
- RQ2 (broader/meta): In [field]'s theoretical discourse, how is theory related to research methodology, paradigm commitments, or research practice, and how does the focal debate make that relation visible?
- Bridge RQ: What methodological/paradigmatic implications are explicitly drawn, implicitly assumed, or absent in the focal debate?

## Required distinctions

Define these terms before finalizing the RQ:

| Term | Working definition |
|------|--------------------|
| Theory | Conceptual framework explaining/constituting the field's objects, actors, institutions, or knowledge claims. |
| Methodology | Logic justifying research object selection, question formation, evidence handling, and analytical procedure. |
| Method | Concrete technique such as content analysis, citation analysis, interviews, classification analysis. |
| Paradigm | Broad assumptions about legitimate problems, objects, evidence, explanations, and research community norms. |
| Research practice | The practice of designing, conducting, and interpreting research; distinguish from professional/practitioner practice unless the RQ explicitly includes that gap. |

## Coding theory-methodology relations

Useful categories for Phase 3 analysis:

| Type | Relation | Description |
|------|----------|-------------|
| T1 | Foundational-prescriptive | Theory is claimed to prescribe research objects, methods, or criteria. |
| T2 | Ontological-delimiting | Theory defines what counts as information, document, knowledge, user, etc. |
| T3 | Heuristic-orienting | Theory guides question formation and attention without directly prescribing method. |
| T4 | Paradigm-constituting | Theory shapes a research community's assumptions and standards. |
| T5 | Retrospective-rationalizing | Theory explains or legitimates existing practice after the fact. |
| T6 | Praxis-dialectical | Theory and research practice mutually form one another. |
| T7 | Weak/absent linkage | A theory is discussed but methodological implications are not specified. |
| T8 | Anti-foundational/skeptical | Foundational theory itself is rejected or treated with suspicion. |

## Devil's Advocate checks

Before passing Phase 1, ask:

1. Is the focal object being accidentally demoted to a mere example?
2. Is the broader RQ making claims that require a wider evidence base than the planned corpus provides?
3. Is the bridge RQ explicit enough to connect the focal debate to the broader theoretical ambition?
4. Are claims framed as analytical/typological implications rather than statistical or exhaustive generalization when the corpus is asymmetrically anchored?
